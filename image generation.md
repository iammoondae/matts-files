# Presentation Image Prompt Generation Framework

## Purpose

This framework provides a structured process for generating high-quality image prompts that support presentation slides. Its purpose is not merely to create attractive images but to produce visuals that strengthen understanding, improve retention, and reinforce learning objectives.

The framework can be applied to presentations used in schools, universities, training programs, workshops, online courses, learning management systems, and self-paced educational content.

The framework is platform-agnostic. It does not depend on a specific presentation software, image generation model, or workflow. Instead, it establishes a repeatable decision-making process that ensures generated images remain relevant, accurate, and educationally valuable.

The ultimate goal is to transform slide content into image prompts that:

* Support learning outcomes
* Improve comprehension
* Increase learner engagement
* Reduce misconceptions
* Maintain consistency throughout a presentation
* Adapt appropriately to the target audience

---

# Core Design Principles

All image prompt generation should follow these principles.

## Educational Value Comes First

Images should contribute directly to learning.

A visually impressive image that does not support the lesson objective is less valuable than a simple image that clearly communicates the intended concept.

Before generating any image prompt, determine what educational purpose the image serves.

Ask:

* What should learners understand after seeing this image?
* How does the image reinforce the lesson?
* Does the image clarify a difficult concept?

If the image does not improve understanding, it should be reconsidered.

---

## Accuracy Is Essential

Educational images must represent concepts correctly.

Visual misinformation can create misconceptions that are difficult to correct later.

Generated images should:

* Reflect accepted academic knowledge
* Use accurate relationships between objects
* Avoid pseudo-scientific representations
* Avoid historical inaccuracies
* Avoid misleading symbolism

Whenever accuracy and artistic creativity conflict, accuracy should take priority.

---

## Age Appropriateness Matters

Different audiences require different levels of detail.

The same topic may require vastly different visual treatments depending on learner age and experience.

Younger learners generally benefit from:

* Simpler compositions
* Larger focal objects
* Fewer details
* Clear visual hierarchy

Older learners can benefit from:

* Greater realism
* More complex relationships
* Scientific precision
* Detailed representations

The image should always match the audience's developmental level.

---

## Clarity Over Complexity

Educational visuals should communicate a primary idea quickly.

Avoid creating images that contain too many competing elements.

When designing prompts:

* Focus on one main concept
* Limit distractions
* Highlight important relationships
* Maintain visual organization

A learner should be able to identify the image's purpose within a few seconds.

---

## Consistency Across the Presentation

Images within the same presentation should feel connected.

Consistency improves professionalism and reduces cognitive load.

Maintain consistency in:

* Level of complexity
* Educational focus
* Subject accuracy
* Visual organization
* Learner appropriateness

Each image should feel like part of a coherent instructional experience.

---

# Slide Analysis Process

Before creating an image prompt, analyze the slide thoroughly.

The quality of the final image depends heavily on the quality of the analysis.

## Step 1: Identify the Main Topic

Determine the central subject of the slide.

Examples:

* Photosynthesis
* Atomic Structure
* Water Cycle
* The French Revolution
* Ecosystems

The main topic becomes the foundation of the image prompt.

---

## Step 2: Identify Key Concepts

Determine the most important concepts learners must understand.

For example:

Photosynthesis may include:

* Sunlight
* Carbon dioxide
* Water
* Glucose
* Oxygen

Not every concept needs equal emphasis.

Identify which concepts are most important for the lesson objective.

---

## Step 3: Identify the Learning Goal

Ask:

"What should learners know, understand, or be able to explain after viewing this slide?"

This question is often more important than the slide title.

Example:

A slide titled "Photosynthesis" may have different goals:

* Identify inputs and outputs
* Explain the process
* Relate photosynthesis to ecosystems

The learning goal influences the type of image required.

---

## Step 4: Identify Visual Opportunities

Determine which information can be communicated visually.

Some concepts are highly visual:

* Anatomy
* Geography
* Physical processes

Others may require symbolic representation:

* Leadership
* Democracy
* Ethics

Select a visual approach that strengthens understanding.

---

## Step 5: Identify Potential Misconceptions

Determine common misunderstandings learners may have.

Examples:

* Electrons orbiting exactly like planets
* Incorrect cell structures
* Oversimplified historical events

The image prompt should actively avoid reinforcing misconceptions.

---

# Educational Intent Classification

Every slide should be categorized according to its educational intent.

The category influences how the image should be generated.

## Concept Explanation

Used when introducing or explaining a concept.

Examples:

* Photosynthesis
* Gravity
* Chemical Bonding

Focus on helping learners understand the core idea.

---

## Process or Sequence

Used when explaining how something happens.

Examples:

* Water Cycle
* Scientific Method
* Digestion

The image should emphasize progression and relationships between stages.

---

## Structure and Parts

Used when teaching components of a larger system.

Examples:

* Plant Cell
* Human Heart
* Computer Hardware

The image should emphasize organization and spatial relationships.

---

## Comparison

Used when examining similarities and differences.

Examples:

* Plant vs Animal Cells
* Renewable vs Nonrenewable Energy
* Mitosis vs Meiosis

The image should clearly distinguish compared elements.

---

## Real-World Application

Used when connecting concepts to authentic situations.

Examples:

* Ecosystems
* Laboratory Safety
* Environmental Issues

The image should demonstrate relevance and context.

---

## Historical or Narrative Context

Used for stories, literature, and historical events.

Examples:

* Historical figures
* Literary scenes
* Cultural practices

The image should accurately represent the setting and context.

---

# Visual Planning

After determining the educational intent, plan the image before generating the prompt.

## Must-Include Elements

Identify essential components.

These are elements without which the image would fail to communicate the lesson.

Example:

Water Cycle:

* Sun
* Clouds
* Water bodies
* Evaporation
* Precipitation

---

## Supporting Elements

These elements strengthen understanding but are not mandatory.

Examples:

* Trees
* Mountains
* Rivers
* Buildings

Supporting elements should never distract from the main concept.

---

## Exclusions

Identify elements that should not appear.

Examples:

* Decorative distractions
* Inaccurate structures
* Unrelated objects
* Misleading symbols

Explicit exclusions improve image quality and educational accuracy.

---

## Focus Hierarchy

Determine which elements deserve the most attention.

Not all objects are equally important.

Prioritize:

1. Learning objective
2. Core concept
3. Supporting context
4. Decorative elements

The most important educational element should dominate the composition.

---

# Age-Level Adaptation

Image complexity should match learner needs.

## Elementary Level

Characteristics:

* Simplified visuals
* Clear focal points
* Limited complexity
* Easy-to-recognize objects

Goals:

* Build familiarity
* Encourage engagement
* Introduce concepts

Avoid excessive detail.

---

## Junior High Level

Characteristics:

* Moderate realism
* More relationships shown
* Increased detail

Goals:

* Deepen understanding
* Strengthen conceptual connections

Balance clarity with accuracy.

---

## Senior High Level

Characteristics:

* Greater realism
* Higher accuracy
* More sophisticated representations

Goals:

* Prepare learners for advanced study
* Support analytical thinking

Include relevant complexity when necessary.

---

## University Level

Characteristics:

* High disciplinary accuracy
* Detailed representations
* Advanced conceptual relationships

Goals:

* Support expert understanding
* Reinforce professional standards

Accuracy becomes increasingly important.

---

# Subject-Specific Guidance

## Science

Science visuals should prioritize evidence-based representations.

Focus on:

* Cause-and-effect relationships
* Scientific accuracy
* Observable phenomena

Avoid speculative or fictional depictions.

---

## Chemistry

Represent:

* Molecular relationships
* Reactions
* Laboratory contexts

Avoid:

* Impossible molecular structures
* Unsafe laboratory practices
* Misleading visual metaphors

---

## Biology

Represent:

* Correct anatomy
* Biological processes
* Appropriate scale relationships

Avoid distortions that alter scientific meaning.

---

## Physics

Represent:

* Forces
* Motion
* Energy relationships

The image should help learners visualize abstract interactions.

---

## Mathematics

Represent:

* Accurate shapes
* Correct proportions
* Meaningful visual relationships

Diagrams should support mathematical reasoning.

---

## Geography

Represent:

* Realistic landforms
* Environmental relationships
* Spatial organization

Avoid inaccurate geographic representations.

---

## History

Represent:

* Appropriate clothing
* Architecture
* Technology
* Cultural context

Historical accuracy should be maintained whenever possible.

---

## Language Arts

Represent:

* Story settings
* Character interactions
* Contextual details

Images should strengthen comprehension rather than replace reading.

---

# Prompt Construction Rules

Once analysis and planning are complete, create the image prompt.

## Start With the Topic

Clearly state what the image is about.

The topic should be immediately identifiable.

---

## State the Learning Focus

Describe what learners should understand from the image.

This helps align visuals with educational goals.

---

## Describe Essential Elements

List the important objects, relationships, and processes that must appear.

Focus on instructional value.

---

## Describe Context

Specify relevant educational context.

Examples:

* Classroom-friendly
* Educational illustration
* Scientific representation
* Historical scene

Context helps maintain instructional relevance.

---

## Specify Clarity Requirements

Include instructions that encourage:

* Clear organization
* Strong visual hierarchy
* Easy interpretation
* Educational usefulness

Avoid unnecessary artistic complexity.

---

# Negative Prompt Development

Negative prompts improve reliability by reducing unwanted outputs.

Negative prompts should discourage:

* Visual clutter
* Distorted anatomy
* Scientific inaccuracies
* Historical inaccuracies
* Unrelated objects
* Excessive decorative elements
* Misleading symbolism
* Watermarks
* Logos
* Low-quality details
* Cropped subjects

The negative prompt should support the learning objective by eliminating distractions.

---

# Quality Assurance Checklist

Before accepting a generated prompt, evaluate it using the following checklist.

## Educational Alignment

Does the image support the slide objective?

If not, revise the prompt.

---

## Accuracy

Is the content scientifically, historically, or mathematically correct?

If uncertain, revise the prompt.

---

## Clarity

Can learners quickly identify the main idea?

If not, simplify the prompt.

---

## Relevance

Does every major visual element contribute to learning?

Remove unnecessary elements.

---

## Age Appropriateness

Does the complexity match the target audience?

Adjust detail accordingly.

---

## Consistency

Does the image fit the rest of the presentation?

Maintain continuity throughout the slide deck.

---

# Master Workflow

The complete process should follow this sequence.

## Stage 1

Analyze slide content.

Identify:

* Main topic
* Key concepts
* Learning goal

---

## Stage 2

Determine educational intent.

Classify the slide as:

* Concept
* Process
* Structure
* Comparison
* Real-world application
* Historical or narrative context

---

## Stage 3

Plan the visual.

Identify:

* Must-include elements
* Supporting elements
* Exclusions
* Focus hierarchy

---

## Stage 4

Adapt to audience level.

Adjust:

* Complexity
* Detail
* Realism
* Accuracy requirements

---

## Stage 5

Apply subject-specific guidance.

Ensure disciplinary accuracy.

---

## Stage 6

Construct the image prompt.

Include:

* Topic
* Learning focus
* Essential elements
* Educational context
* Clarity requirements

---

## Stage 7

Construct the negative prompt.

Remove distractions and inaccuracies.

---

## Stage 8

Perform quality assurance.

Verify:

* Educational alignment
* Accuracy
* Clarity
* Relevance
* Consistency

---

## Stage 9

Finalize the prompt.

Only release the image prompt after all checks have been completed.

---

# Conclusion

Effective educational image generation begins long before a prompt is written. The most important decisions occur during analysis, planning, classification, and validation. By following this framework, image prompts become more than descriptions for an image generator; they become instructional tools that support learning objectives, reinforce key concepts, and improve the overall quality of presentations.

The framework should always prioritize educational value, accuracy, clarity, age appropriateness, and consistency. When these principles guide every decision, generated images become meaningful learning assets rather than decorative additions.
